Lesson one: Number Lines



This is a number line. It goes from -10 to 10. Notice that when we are going to the right, every time we go over a gap, our number gets bigger by one. The opposite is true when we are going to the left.

Now let's try out some practice questions. How much bigger is 10 compared to 3?













It's 7 bigger, because you have to go over seven gaps from 3 to get to 10 while going to the right!


How much smaller is 5 compared to 8?













It's 3 smaller, because you have to go over 3 gaps from 8 to get to 5 while going to the left!

The questions we did were about finding the difference between numbers, which is also called subtraction. Let's start doing some slightly different questions!

If you start at 3, and you go over 4 gaps while going to the right, what number do you get to?













You get to 7!


What we just did there is finding two values combined together! This is also called addition.






Understanding addition and subtraction

Remember how we did our number lines? This is an extension of that knowledge!

We will write addition and subtraction the normal way now. For instance, starting at 3 and going over 4 gaps while going to the right and getting 7 is written as 3+4=7. Finding the difference of 10 and 3 and getting 7 is written as 10-3=7. The difference is just how much bigger or smaller a number is compared to another number. For example: How much bigger is 3 than 1? Can be written as: What is 3-1? You can also understand it like this: Subtraction is like starting with the 1st number and going a certain amount of gaps to the left. That amount is the second number. And what you get is the third number, the number which is to the right of the two-line symbol, the equals sign. But if the first number is smaller than the second one, then we would have to use the negative sign. For instance, 3-10=-7. The negative sign is really just a little line. See those numbers to the left of 0? Those are negative numbers. -7, for instance, means the number that is 7 less than 0.

Let's do some practice now! What is 3+6? What is 2+3? Here's a hard one! What's -3-4?













The answers are: 9, 5, and -7!






Understanding bigger numbers

Let's understand some bigger numbers! First of all, let's understand the tens digit. We write ten as '10'. In a 2-digit number, the first number represents how many tens there are in the number, and the second represents how many ones there are. In a one-digit number there is only the digit that represents how many ones there are.






Addition and subtraction with 2-digit numbers plus single-digit numbers (no carrying)

Now that we understand bigger numbers, let's use them to do simple addition and subtraction!

Let's first explain 55-5. When we are subtracting a two-digit number by a single-digit number without carrying [we will learn that later], we subtract the digits that represent how many ones there are [the ones digits]. That is our new ones digit, and we keep our old digit that represents how many tens there are in a number [the tens digit]. So, 55-5=50. Now, let's explain 55+4. You do the same thing with adding a 2-digit number with a single-digit number without carrying but you add the ones digits instead of subtracting them. So, 55+4=59.
Here are some practice questions. What's 45-4? What's 36-3? What's 53+3?













Answers are 41 for Q1, 33 for Q2 and 56 for the third question.
